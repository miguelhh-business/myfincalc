First Home Buyer Guide: How Much Do You Need to Save?

📅 March 2026🕐 10 min read🇳🇿 🇦🇺 🇬🇧 🇺🇸

Buying your first home is the biggest financial commitment most people ever make. The deposit alone can take years to save, and that is before you factor in legal fees, inspections, insurance, and the ongoing costs of ownership. This guide covers the real deposit requirements across four countries, the government schemes available to help, and how to build a realistic savings plan.

Why 20% matters: Below 20%, most lenders charge Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI) or similar — an extra cost of thousands of dollars that protects the bank, not you. Government schemes in several countries help first home buyers avoid this.

🇳🇿 New Zealand first home buyers

KiwiSaver First Home Withdrawal

After contributing to KiwiSaver for 3+ years, you can withdraw your employee contributions, employer contributions, and investment returns for a first home. The government member tax credit portion stays in the fund. There is no house price cap on this withdrawal, making it the most flexible tool available to NZ first home buyers.

First Home Grant

Provides up to $5,000 per person for existing homes or $10,000 per person for new builds. A couple buying a new build can receive up to $20,000. Requirements include 3+ years of KiwiSaver contributions, income under $95,000 (single) or $150,000 (couple), and house price under the regional cap. The Auckland cap is $875,000 — which limits usefulness in much of Auckland where the median exceeds $1 million.

🇦🇺 Australian first home buyers

First Home Guarantee: Allows eligible first home buyers to purchase with as little as 5% deposit without paying LMI, as the government guarantees the remaining 15%. Income caps apply (A$125,000 single, A$200,000 combined). Property price caps vary by state.

First Home Owner Grant (FHOG): Varies by state. In NSW: $10,000 for new homes under $750,000. In QLD: $30,000 for new homes. In VIC: $10,000 for homes under $750,000. These grants generally apply only to new builds or substantially renovated homes.

First Home Super Saver Scheme (FHSSS): Allows you to save for a deposit inside super, where contributions are taxed at 15% instead of your marginal rate. Maximum withdrawal of $50,000 in voluntary contributions plus associated earnings.

🇬🇧 UK first home buyers

Lifetime ISA (LISA): Save up to £4,000 per year and the government adds a 25% bonus (up to £1,000/year). Available to 18-39 year olds. The property must cost under £450,000. Withdrawals for non-housing purposes before age 60 incur a 25% penalty.

Mortgage Guarantee Scheme: Allows 5% deposit mortgages from participating lenders with the government guaranteeing a portion of the loan. Available on properties up to £600,000.

Stamp Duty relief: First-time buyers in England and Northern Ireland pay no Stamp Duty on properties up to £300,000, and a reduced rate up to £500,000.

🇺🇸 US first home buyers

FHA loans: Allow deposits as low as 3.5% with mortgage insurance. Available to all buyers (not just first-timers) with credit scores of 580+.

Conventional loans: Some programs allow 3% down for first-time buyers. Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required below 20% but can be removed once you reach 20% equity.

Down Payment Assistance programs: Many states and cities offer grants or low-interest loans for down payment assistance. These vary widely by location and change frequently.

How to save your deposit faster

Set a specific target and deadline. A vague goal of "saving for a house" is less effective than "$80,000 by December 2028." Break it into monthly milestones and track progress visually.

Automate your savings. Set up an automatic transfer on payday to a separate high-yield savings account. If the money moves before you see it, you will not miss it.

Use a high-yield savings account. At 4-5% interest, $50,000 earns $2,000-$2,500 per year in interest. Over a 3-year saving period, that is an extra $7,000-$8,000 in free money.

Maximise KiwiSaver (NZ) or FHSSS (AU). Employer matching and government contributions accelerate your savings. In NZ, 3% employer match on a $75,000 salary adds $2,250/year to your deposit fund.

Consider house-hacking. Buying a multi-unit property (like a duplex) and living in one unit while renting the others can offset mortgage costs significantly. This works particularly well for first purchases.

Frequently asked questions

How much deposit do I need for a first home?
20% is standard for the best mortgage rates and to avoid LMI. However, most countries offer first home buyer schemes allowing 5-10% deposits. In the US, FHA loans allow as low as 3.5%. Government guarantees can eliminate LMI even with smaller deposits.
Can I use KiwiSaver for a house deposit?
Yes. After 3+ years of contributions, you can withdraw your employee and employer contributions plus investment returns for a first home purchase. There is no house price cap on this withdrawal. You must leave $1,000 in your KiwiSaver account.
What is the First Home Grant in NZ?
Up to $5,000/person for existing homes or $10,000/person for new builds ($20,000 max for a couple buying new). Requires 3+ years KiwiSaver contributions, income under $95,000 (single) or $150,000 (couple), and house price under regional caps ($875,000 in Auckland).
Is it worth buying with less than 20% deposit?
It depends. A smaller deposit gets you into the market sooner but costs more through LMI (unless government-guaranteed) and higher interest rates. If house prices are rising, getting in sooner can offset these costs. If prices are flat, waiting to reach 20% saves money. Government guarantee schemes that waive LMI make low deposits much more attractive.
What hidden costs should first home buyers budget for?
Budget an extra $5,000-$10,000 beyond your deposit for: legal/conveyancing ($1,500-$3,000), building inspection ($400-$800), LIM report ($300-$400 in NZ), loan establishment fees, moving costs, and immediate maintenance or furnishing. These costs catch many first-time buyers off guard.
