How Much Should I Invest Each Month? (Based on Your Goals)

📅 March 2026🕐 9 min read🎯 Practical

The most common investing question is not "what should I invest in?" — it is "how much should I invest?" The answer depends entirely on your goals, timeline, and current financial situation. This guide gives you concrete numbers to work with.

The 50/30/20 rule as a starting point

The 50/30/20 budgeting framework allocates your after-tax income into three categories: 50% to needs (housing, food, utilities, insurance, transport), 30% to wants (dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, hobbies), and 20% to savings and investing (retirement contributions, emergency fund, extra debt payments, investments).

On a $75,000 after-tax income, 20% means investing $15,000 per year or $1,250 per month. This is a starting point — not a rule. Some people invest 10% and that is fine. Others pursuing FIRE invest 50-70%.

The priority order for your money

Before investing aggressively, make sure your financial foundations are solid. Here is the recommended priority order:

Priority 1: Employer retirement match. If your employer matches KiwiSaver, 401(k), or super contributions, contribute enough to capture the full match. A 3% employer match on a $75,000 salary is $2,250/year in free money — a guaranteed 100% return. Never leave this on the table, even if you have debt.

Priority 2: High-interest debt. Pay off any debt with an interest rate above 7-8% before investing beyond the employer match. The guaranteed return from paying off a 20% credit card exceeds any expected investment return.

Priority 3: Emergency fund. Build 3-6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield savings account before investing aggressively. Without this buffer, any emergency forces you to sell investments (possibly at a loss) or take on debt.

Priority 4: Invest everything else. Once the match is captured, high-interest debt is gone, and your emergency fund is solid, invest as much as you can toward your goals. Max out tax-advantaged accounts first (KiwiSaver above match, IRA, TFSA), then invest in taxable brokerage accounts.

Lump sum vs dollar-cost averaging

If you have a lump sum to invest (inheritance, bonus, savings), should you invest it all at once or spread it out over months?

Research consistently shows that lump sum investing outperforms dollar-cost averaging about two-thirds of the time. This makes sense because markets go up more often than they go down, so waiting means missing out on returns.

However, dollar-cost averaging (investing a fixed amount regularly) has real advantages: it removes emotion from the equation, reduces the risk of investing everything at a market peak, and is more practical for most people who invest from their salary rather than lump sums.

The best approach for most people: invest your regular monthly amount via dollar-cost averaging, and if you receive a windfall, invest at least half immediately and spread the rest over 3-6 months if the lump sum makes you nervous.

How to invest more without earning more

Track your spending for one month. Most people find 10-20% of their spending is on things they do not value. Redirecting even $200/month from low-value spending to investing adds $98,000 over 20 years at 7% returns.

Automate on payday. Set up automatic transfers to your investment account on the day you get paid. If the money moves before you see it in your spending account, you will not miss it. This is the single most effective behavioural trick for investing consistently.

Invest raises and bonuses. When your income increases, invest at least half the increase before your lifestyle adjusts. A $5,000 raise where you invest $2,500/year adds $130,000 over 20 years.

Start small, increase gradually. Investing $100/month is better than investing nothing while waiting until you can afford $500/month. Start where you are and increase by $50 every 6 months. You will barely notice each individual increase.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I invest each month?
A common starting point is 15-20% of gross income. On a $75,000 salary, that is $938-$1,250/month. However, the right amount depends on your specific goals and timeline. Someone targeting early retirement may invest 40-50%+. Someone paying off debt may invest only the employer match temporarily.
What is the 50/30/20 rule?
50% of after-tax income to needs (housing, food, utilities), 30% to wants (dining, entertainment), and 20% to savings and investments. It is a guideline, not a rule — adjust based on your priorities. High earners can often invest 30-40% by keeping wants below 20%.
Should I invest or pay off debt first?
Always capture employer retirement match first (guaranteed 100% return). Then pay off debt above 7-8% interest. Then build emergency fund. Then invest everything else. Low-interest debt (mortgage under 5%) can coexist with investing since market returns typically exceed the interest rate.
Is $100/month enough to start investing?
Yes. $100/month at 7% for 30 years grows to about $122,000. Starting small and being consistent matters far more than waiting until you can afford a larger amount. Increase your contributions by $50 every 6 months as your income grows.
Should I invest a lump sum or spread it out?
Research shows lump sum outperforms about 67% of the time because markets trend upward. But if a large lump sum makes you anxious, invest half immediately and spread the rest over 3-6 months. The important thing is that the money gets invested rather than sitting in cash.
